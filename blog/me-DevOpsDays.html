DevOps Days as a Newbie

Into the Wilds of the Unknown

August 26, 2015

DevOps Days Chicago is a tech conference focused on the development to operations side of the tech world. This is where everyone who deals with deploying, servers, security, testing, and all of the systems needed to get code and the things created by code out the door and into use. It breaks the walls between creating the code and getting it out the door, asking both sides to work together. It also breaks the waves of deploying code into a river that streams out the door. In DevOps, deploying code is a continual process.

DevOps Days Chicago was my first tech conference and an ambitious one at that. I was in Phase 0 of Dev Bootcamp (so at the very beginning of a code school) and had yet to write enough code to deploy, let alone deploy continuously. Later, I was at a different event and was talking about what I learned at DevOps days and connected it to the topic at hand and I was told not to worry about it...but I wanted to worry about it. I want to touch my hands in the creation and manipulation of massive code bases, and I want my code to be deployed. Sometimes, life hands you opportunities in weird order, so I walked into the Chicago DevOps conference without a job in tech, in the beginning of code school, alone, were I was assured to be the odd person out by being female and having to google devops the day beforehand. I walked and learned so much from the experience.

So, what did this newbie learn? Many things:

  • First, there's something called "yak shaving", which means to fix one item, which breaks another, and then so back to fix it again (probably best to google that definition).
  • I watched an amazingly smooth and informative talk about the Orbitz deployment process. They have an impressing continuous deployment system that looks like an automated factory in the slides.
  • Don't praise unhealthy behavior! The fact of the team staying until 5am to fix issues repeatedly should not be praised as hero effort. Yes, fixing issues is good but disrupting a person's sleep cycle is not good. Why are you praising unhealthy behavour? If you value your team, stop asking them to harm their physical health, mental health, and social lives. Make the system better so you don't have to call them in, no one needs to create heroes.
  • DevOps teams for different size companies require different skill-sets, hire for the skills needed, not the resume desired.
  • Stop fearing failure.
  • Stop fearing failure.
  • Stop fearing failure.
  • Make a safe place to do post-mortems.
  • Professional men still get a kick out of flying the bird. Why? I think they aren't secure enough to use rainbows and unicorns. Shock value is still shock value, I just can't remember any other significant part of the talk (it's also been a few weeks).
  • Feedback is important. Give useful feedback, and thank people who give it to you. It's not an easy thing to do.
  • Take the blame out of feedback; state what happened (without assumptions on why) and how it affected your work.
  • Monitor, set up your monitoring and use it.
  • Monitoring is how you find the weak spots.
  • Monitor so the issues are caught before your customers find them.
  • Automate as much as you can in your production pipeline.
  • Write tests to check and monitor the code being deployed.
  • There is something called the Phoenix Project, read it.
  • Companies need to think: do I have enough to need a DevOps team or do I need engineers to be good at it.
  • There are no unicorns.
  • The audience really appreciated the opportunity to talk about feedback. The room full of mostly guys needed and appreciated the chance to listen, talk and think about this topic. Soft skills might be the hardest to master.

These are the points which stuck in my head in a more permanent fashion. Other attendees might have walked away with deeper, more detailed knowledge (remember: I was starting from the zero point).

All the talks were recorded and are available to watch to your hearts content.

One of my goals for the conference was to learn what a new dev needed to know about devops and the operations side of a tech team. Here's what I learned in a nutshell to take forward: Make the devops or operations team your friend. Talk to them outside of big deploys and include them in discussions of the code. Work together to get as much automation in testing and deployment as possible, so no one comes in on the weekend unexpectedly. If code doesn't get out the door (or get out the door broken), it's everyone's problem, not just the operations side, so make them your friends at work.
